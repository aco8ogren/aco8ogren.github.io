Falling leaves

‚Üê prev

These leaves follow real aerodynamics, simulated in real time

The falling leaves in the background are a physics-based simulation that I built as a fun way to introduce myself to JavaScript. Falling leaves are visually simple and elegant, yet represent an intricate mathematical dance happening in the background. Here, you can take a peek behind the curtain to understand a bit more about how one might model this beautiful natural process.

leaf landscape (pure ambience)
leaf lab (engaging and informative)

The big picture

In this simulation, each leaf is reacting to wind and gravity. By knowing how fast the surrounding air is moving relative to each leaf, we can determine how the leaf will move and turn over time.

a more technical overview

Each leaf, idealized as a thin disk, responds to wind and gravity. The air's velocity field is a space-time varying sinusoid combined additively with randomly-spawning Gaussian-enveloped vortices. By knowing the relative velocity between each leaf and the surrounding air, we can compute the forces and moments that act upon each leaf. From there, we can apply Newton's second law and integrate to propagate the state of each leaf over time. When a leaf wanders too far out of bounds, it fades and de-spawns - then spawns a new leaf at the top of the page.

symbols, coordinate systems, units, and conventions

Except where stated otherwise, everything runs on the $\left[\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}\cdot\text{s}\right]$ unit system.
$\uvec{i}, \uvec{j}, \uvec{k}$ are unit vectors in the $x, y, z$ directions, respectively
In website space, $\uvec{i}, \uvec{j}, \uvec{k}$ point right, up, and into the page, respectively
Bold symbols such as $\vec{v}$ are vectors
Hatted symbols such as $\uvec{v}$ are unit vectors
$t$ is time
$\mathbf{x} = [x,y,z]$ denote spatial coordinates
$\omega$ is a frequency in $[1/s]$
$\gamma$ is a wavevector (spatial version of frequency) in $[1/m]$
$\propto$ means "proportional to" (i.e. I'm omitting a scaling factor)
$\rho$ signifies density (could be an area density $\left[\text{kg}/\text{m}^2\right]$ or volumetric density $\left[\text{kg}/\text{m}^3\right]$)

Kinematics

Kinematics is the study and description of motion ‚Äî positions, orientations, velocities, and accelerations of objects as functions of time. To properly describe how a leaf falls through the breeze, we need to simulate both the linear kinematics (how the leaf is moving in $x, y, z$) and the rotational kinematics (how the leaf flips, spins, and rotates).

show the math: kinematics

Linear kinematics are pretty simple in this simulation. Each leaf has a position, velocity, and acceleration: $\vec{x}_{\text{leaf}}$, $\vec{v}_{\text{leaf}}$, and $\vec{a}_{\text{leaf}}.$

Rotational kinematics typically require a bit more effort to understand ‚Äî but seeing the leaves rotate is so worth it! We use quaternions to track rotational kinematics.

show the math: quaternion definitions

A quaternion is an ordered pair \(q=(w,\vec{q})\in\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}^3\) with scalar part \(w\) and vector part \(\vec{q}\). We write the quaternion product as \(\otimes\).

Addition & scalar multiplication

$$ (w_1,\vec{q}_1) + (w_2,\vec{q}_2) = (w_1{+}w_2,\; \vec{q}_1{+}\vec{q}_2),\qquad \alpha(w,\vec{q}) = (\alpha w,\; \alpha\vec{q}). $$

Hamilton product (non-commutative)

$$ (a,\vec{A}) \otimes (b,\vec{B}) = \Big(ab - \vec{A}\!\cdot\!\vec{B},\;\; a\,\vec{B} + b\,\vec{A} + \vec{A}\times\vec{B}\Big). $$

Conjugate, norm, inverse

$$ q^*=(w,-\vec{q}),\quad \|q\|^2 = q\otimes q^* = w^2 + \|\vec{q}\|^2,\quad q^{-1} = \frac{q^*}{\|q\|^2}\ \ (q\neq 0). $$
show the math: unit quaternions & rotations

A unit quaternion satisfies \(\|q\|=1\). Unit quaternions parametrize 3D rotations; \(q\) and \(-q\) represent the same rotation (i.e. quaternions double cover of the space of all possible 3D rotations, \(SO(3)\)).

Rotate a vector by \(q\)

Embed \(\vec{v}\in\mathbb{R}^3\) as a pure quaternion \((0,\vec{v})\) and sandwich:

$$ \mathcal{R}_q(\vec{v}) \;=\; \big(q \otimes (0,\vec{v}) \otimes q^{-1}\big)_{\text{vector part}}. $$

Composition order

Applying \(q_1\) then \(q_2\) equals \(q_2 \otimes q_1\) (left-acting in the sandwich).

show the math: angular velocity ‚Üí quaternion kinematics

Let \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\) be angular velocity. The quaternion ODE depends on the frame of \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\).

World (inertial) frame angular velocity \(\boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathrm{W}}\)

$$ \dot{q} = \tfrac12\,(0,\boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathrm{W}})\ \otimes\ q, \qquad q_{t+\Delta t} = \exp\!\Big(\tfrac12\,(0,\boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathrm{W}})\,\Delta t\Big)\ \otimes\ q_t. $$

Body (leaf) frame angular velocity \(\boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathrm{L}}\)

$$ \dot{q} = \tfrac12\, q \ \otimes\ (0,\boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathrm{L}}), \qquad q_{t+\Delta t} = q_t\ \otimes\ \exp\!\Big(\tfrac12\,(0,\boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathrm{L}})\,\Delta t\Big). $$

Small-step first-order increment (approx.)

$$ \Delta q \approx \big(1,\ \tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\omega}\,\Delta t\big),\quad q_{t+\Delta t} \approx \Delta q \otimes q_t\ \text{(world)}\ \ \text{or}\ \ q_t \otimes \Delta q\ \text{(body)}. $$

Numerics: use the exponential form (axis‚Äìangle) for finite steps and renormalize \(q\) after each update to control drift.

show the math: quaternions

A unit quaternion \(q=(w,\vec{q})\in\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}^3\) encodes a 3D rotation: \(w^2+\|\vec{q}\|^2=1\). Rotating a vector \(\vec{v}\) by \(q\) is ‚Äúsandwich‚Äù multiplication:

$$\mathcal{R}_q(\vec{v}) = q\,(0,\vec{v})\,q^{-1}.$$

For an axis‚Äìangle rotation of angle \(\theta\) about unit axis \(\uvec{u}\),

$$q(\uvec{u},\theta)=\Big(\cos\tfrac{\theta}{2},\; \uvec{u}\,\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}\Big).$$

Composition is quaternion product: applying \(q_1\) then \(q_2\) equals \(q_2\otimes q_1\). The inverse is \(q^{-1}=(w,-\vec{q})\) for unit \(q\).

show the math: frames & how \(q\) acts

We interpret \(q\) as the rotation that maps leaf-local vectors into world space: \( {}^{\mathrm{W}}\vec{v}=\mathcal{R}_q\!\left({}^{\mathrm{L}}\vec{v}\right)\). Conversely, \( {}^{\mathrm{L}}\vec{v}=\mathcal{R}_{q^{-1}}\!\left({}^{\mathrm{W}}\vec{v}\right)\).

In this sim the leaf mesh‚Äôs local normal is \( {}^{\mathrm{L}}\uvec{k}=(0,0,1)\). The world normal is therefore

$$\uvec{n} = \mathcal{R}_q\!\big({}^{\mathrm{L}}\uvec{k}\big).$$

When we need an in-plane lever arm for the center of pressure (CoP), we drop the out-of-plane component in the leaf frame, scale, then rotate back:

  1. Project \( {}^{\mathrm{L}}\hat{\vec{v}} = \text{normalize}\!\left(\big[\mathcal{R}_{q^{-1}}(\hat{\vec{v}}_{\mathrm{rel}})\big]_{\parallel\text{leaf}}\right)\).
  2. \( {}^{\mathrm{L}}\vec{r} = -a_{\mathrm{CoP}}\,R\,{}^{\mathrm{L}}\hat{\vec{v}}\).
  3. \( {}^{\mathrm{W}}\vec{r} = \mathcal{R}_q({}^{\mathrm{L}}\vec{r})\).
  4. \( \boldsymbol{M}_{\mathrm{aero}} = {}^{\mathrm{W}}\vec{r}\times \vec{F}_{\mathrm{aero}}\).
show the math: advancing \(q\) with \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\)

Let \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\) be the world-frame angular velocity. Over a small step \(\Delta t\), the exact rigid-body update is the exponential map:

$$q_{t+\Delta t} = \exp\!\Big(\tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\omega}_t\,\Delta t\Big)\ \otimes\ q_t.$$

With \(\theta=\|\boldsymbol{\omega}\|\Delta t\) and \(\uvec{u}=\boldsymbol{\omega}/\|\boldsymbol{\omega}\|\), the increment is an axis‚Äìangle quaternion

$$\Delta q=\Big(\cos\tfrac{\theta}{2},\ \uvec{u}\,\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}\Big),\qquad q_{t+\Delta t}=\text{normalize}\!\big(\Delta q\otimes q_t\big).$$

This matches the implementation: build \(\Delta q\) from the world-axis \(\uvec{u}\), then premultiply the current \(q\) and renormalize (to keep \(\|q\|=1\)).

show the math: one fixed step

Linear (symplectic Euler):

$$\vec{a}=\tfrac{\vec{F}}{m},\quad \vec{v}_{t+\Delta t}=\vec{v}_t+\vec{a}\Delta t,\quad \vec{x}_{t+\Delta t}=\vec{x}_t+\vec{v}_{t+\Delta t}\Delta t.$$

Rotational (thin disk, scalar \(I\), world-frame):

$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}=\tfrac{\boldsymbol{M}}{I},\quad \boldsymbol{\omega}_{t+\Delta t}=\boldsymbol{\omega}_t+\boldsymbol{\alpha}\Delta t,$$ $$q_{t+\Delta t}=\text{normalize}\!\Big(\exp\!\big(\tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t+\Delta t}\Delta t\big)\otimes q_t\Big),\quad \uvec{n}_{t+\Delta t}=\mathcal{R}_{q_{t+\Delta t}}({}^{\mathrm{L}}\uvec{k}).$$

That‚Äôs exactly what the code does each substep.

Aerodynamics

Wind

Wind is just air that's moving. But in real life, air movement isn't the same everywhere, and it doesn't always move in the same way. The wind in this simulation reflects that real-life variation ‚Äî it's a function of both space and time.

In this sim, I defined the air velocity as a space-time varying sinusoid $(\vec{v}_{\text{background}})$ with randomly-spawning transient vortices $(\vec{v}_{\text{swirl}})$ layered on top.

show the math: air velocity

The background field is a space-time varying sinusoid:
$$\vec{v}_{\text{background}}(\mathbf{x},t)\propto\sin(2\pi\gamma\,y)\sin(2\pi\omega\,t)\uvec{i}.$$

Each swirl is a Gaussian vortex about the $\uvec{k}$-axis, with on-off behavior modulated by smoothed heaviside envelopes: $$\vec{v}_{\text{swirl}}(\mathbf{x},t)\propto e^{-\tfrac12(r/R)^2}\operatorname{env}(t)\uvec{\phi}, \quad\uvec{\phi}=\uvec{k}\times\uvec{r}.$$ where $\operatorname{env}(t,t_{0},t_{1}) = \operatorname{H_{s,2}}(t-t_{0})\operatorname{H_{s,2}}(t_{1} - t)$ turns the vortex on and off with a smoothed heaviside function at times $t_{0}$ and $t_{1}$ respectively. Here, position vector $\vec{r} = \vec{x}-\vec{x_{\text{swirl}}}$, where $\vec{x_{\text{swirl}}}$ is the center of the swirl. Distance from the swirl center is $r = \norm{\vec{r}}$. Radial size variable $R$ scales the spatial size of the swirl.

The total velocity field is the background field, plus any existing swirls: $$\vec{v}_{\text{air}}(\vec{x},t) = \vec{v}_{\text{background}}(\vec{x},t) + \sum_{\text{swirls}}{\vec{v}_{\text{swirl}}(\vec{x},t)}.$$

Leaves

The relative ambient air velocity dictates how each leaf will move. If a leaf is already moving the same way as the surrounding air, it'll just continue to go with the flow ‚Äî no extra effect from the wind. If not, the wind will encourage the leaf to change what it's doing, causing it to drift, lift, float, and tumble.

show the math: forces on a leaf

The relative velocity between the air and each leaf is computed as:

$$\vec{v}_{\text{rel}} = \vec{v_{\text{air}}} - \vec{v_{\text{leaf}}}$$

By idealizing each leaf as a thin disk, we can compute the aerodynamic forces and moments analytically. The aerodynamic force is computed as:

$$\vec{F}_{\mathrm{aero}} = -\frac{1}{2}\,\rho_{\text{air}}\,A_{\text{leaf}}\,\norm{\vec{v}_{\text{rel}}}^2\!\left[ C_D(\theta)\,\uvec{v}_{\text{rel}} + C_N(\theta)\,\operatorname{sgn}\!\big(\uvec{n}_{\text{leaf}}\!\cdot\!\uvec{v}_{\text{rel}}\big)\,\uvec{n}_{\text{leaf}} \right].$$

The coefficients of drag depend on the angle $\theta$ between the relative velocity $(\uvec{v}_{\text{rel}})$ and the leaf normal $(\uvec{n}_{\text{leaf}})$:

$$C_D(\theta)=C_{d,\parallel}+\big(C_{d,\perp}-C_{d,\parallel}\big)\cos^2\!\theta,\qquad C_N(\theta)=C_{N,\max}\sin(2\theta).$$

We offset the center of pressure a small fraction of the radius along the in-plane flow direction, then take a cross product:

  1. Express \(\hat{\mathbf{v}}\) in the leaf‚Äôs local frame via \(q^{-1}\), drop out-of-plane component, normalize.
  2. \(\mathbf{r}_{\text{local}} = -a_{\mathrm{CoP}}R\,\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\parallel}\).
  3. Rotate back to world: \(\mathbf{r} = q\,\mathbf{r}_{\text{local}}\).
  4. \(\boldsymbol{M}_{\mathrm{aero}} = \mathbf{r}\times \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{aero}}\).

Motion (time stepping)

For every leaf, forces update velocity; torques update spin. I use a small fixed time step and a symplectic Euler update so it behaves consistently across frame rates.

show the math: symplectic Euler + quaternion update

Linear:

$$\mathbf{v}_{t+\Delta t}=\mathbf{v}_t+\frac{\mathbf{F}}{m}\Delta t,\qquad \mathbf{x}_{t+\Delta t}=\mathbf{x}_t+\mathbf{v}_{t+\Delta t}\Delta t.$$

Rotational (thin disk, scalar \(I\)):

$$\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t+\Delta t}=\boldsymbol{\omega}_t+\frac{\boldsymbol{M}}{I}\Delta t.$$

Quaternion advance via axis‚Äìangle with normalization:

$$q_{t+\Delta t}=\operatorname{normalize}\!\Big(\exp\!\big(\tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t+\Delta t}\Delta t\big)\otimes q_t\Big).$$

This mirrors the code: velocity-first position update, angular step, premultiply quaternion, then refresh the normal.

The wind

The breeze is the sum of a gentle background that varies with height and time, plus short-lived swirls (little eddies) that ease in, drift, and ease out.

show the math: background breeze

I use a simple separable field along \(x\):

$$\mathbf{u}_{\text{bg}}(x,y,t)=\big[\,U\sin(2\pi\gamma\,y)\sin(2\pi\omega\,t),\,0,\,0\big].$$

That‚Äôs exactly what the background callback returns.

show the math: localized swirls

Each swirl is a Gaussian vortex around axis \(\hat{\mathbf{k}}\) with a smooth life envelope:

Speed profile and direction:

$$\mathbf{u}_{\text{swirl}}(\mathbf{x})=U_0\,e^{-\tfrac12(r/R)^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{t}},\quad \hat{\mathbf{t}}=\hat{\mathbf{k}}\times\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{\perp}.$$

Envelope \( \ell(t)\in[0,1]\) rises and falls via a smoothstep-like polynomial, so swirls appear/disappear without pops. That matches the implemented flc2hs and the swirl tick().

Spawning & culling

I aim for a comfy number of leaves. When a leaf drifts outside a padded border, it fades out and respawns near the top. New leaves arrive via a Poisson process that scales with the current deficit.

show the math: spawn rate & fade

Let \(N_{\max}\) be the target count and \(N\) current. Occupancy deficit \(d=\max(0,N_{\max}-N)/N_{\max}\). Spawn intensity:

$$\lambda_{\text{eff}} = \lambda_0\,d,\qquad k\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda_{\text{eff}}\Delta t).$$

Fade (discrete first-order):

$$\alpha_{t+\Delta t}= \begin{cases} \min(1,\ \alpha_t + s_{\text{in}}\Delta t),&\text{fade in}\\[2pt] \max(0,\ \alpha_t - s_{\text{out}}\Delta t),&\text{fade out} \end{cases}$$

This is exactly how the sim spawns/culls and steps \(\alpha\).

Randomness (but tasteful)

Randomized: initial position (with top-heavy respawns), orientation, mesh choice, color family, and swirl sizes/speeds/lifetimes. Enough variation to feel natural, without chaos.

show the math: distributions used
  • Position: uniform within a padded rectangular domain; respawns biased to the top band.
  • Orientation: random unit vector for the normal; quaternion set from \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\to\mathbf{n}\).
  • Swirl \(R,U_0,\text{duration}\): linear interpolation between min/max with uniform \(u\in[0,1]\).
  • Mesh & palette: random pick from loaded GLBs and material banks.

Rendering & look

Three.js scene with warm sunset lighting and exponential fog so the horizon melts into the page. Leaves are thin, double-sided, transparent materials with depth-write off to play nice in a crowd.

show the math: fog at a glance

Exponential fog roughly scales as \(e^{-\beta z}\) (I use FogExp2). That‚Äôs enough to hint at depth without a heavy post-process.

Camera & UX

The camera gently follows your scrolling: horizontal and vertical scrollers map to a pan window over the simulated space. Movement eases with a simple first-order lag, so it feels calm.

show the math: scroll ‚Üí camera & damping

Scroll progress \(p_x,p_y\in[0,1]\) maps into a pan window \([x_{\min},x_{\max}]\times[y_{\min},y_{\max}]\):

$$x_\star = x_{\text{base}} + s_x\,p_x\,(x_{\max}-x_{\min}),\quad y_\star = y_{\text{base}} + s_y\,p_y\,(y_{\max}-y_{\min}).$$

Damped follow (exact discrete form for a 1st-order system):

$$x_{t+\Delta t} = x_\star + (x_t-x_\star)\,e^{-\lambda\Delta t},\quad y_{t+\Delta t} = y_\star + (y_t-y_\star)\,e^{-\lambda\Delta t}.$$

Matches the View mapping and THREE.MathUtils.damp update used in syncCameraToScroll.

Debug overlays

Press Q/H/Z to toggle quiver heads/visibility and the HUD. The quiver is an instanced grid; the HUD prints live stats. (I keep these off by default so the first impression stays clean.)

show the math: quiver sampling

Uniform grid of \(n_x\times n_y\) points; at each base \(\mathbf{p}_{ij}\) the arrow length is \( \min(L_{\max},\,s\|\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{p}_{ij})\|)\) and direction \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\). Instances are cylinders+cones oriented via the quaternion from a tilted \(\hat{\mathbf{y}}\) to \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\).

Artwork pipeline

Leaves start as SVGs. I triangulate them (ear-clipping), export OBJ/GLB, and load them once. Each spawned leaf instantiates shared geometry with a fresh material from a seasonal palette.

show the math: triangulation sketch

For a simple polygon \(P\), ear-clipping removes convex ‚Äúears‚Äù \( (v_{i-1},v_i,v_{i+1})\) that contain no other vertices, producing triangles in \(O(n^2)\). That‚Äôs what my build script does before writing the GLB.

Performance notes

Fixed physics step at 120 Hz with a frame-time accumulator and a cap on substeps; camera/pan updates honor reduced motion; charts are throttled. Materials share geometry and most textures; leaf fades avoid expensive object churn.

show the math: fixed-step loop

With step \( \Delta t \) and frame time \( \delta\), accumulate \(a\leftarrow a+\delta\); do \(n=\min(\lfloor a/\Delta t\rfloor, N_{\max})\) substeps; \(a\leftarrow a-n\Delta t\). (See your 120 Hz step, 24 substep cap.)

How does it work?

show the math: forces & moments on a leaf

Relative air speed: \( \mathbf{v}_r = \mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t) \), \( V=\lVert \mathbf{v}_r\rVert \), \( \hat{\mathbf{v}}=\mathbf{v}_r/V \), leaf normal \( \mathbf{n} \), and angle \( \theta = \arccos\!\big(\lvert \mathbf{n}\!\cdot\!\hat{\mathbf{v}}\rvert\big) \).

Aerodynamic force split into along-flow (drag) and normal (lift-like):

$$\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{aero}} = -\tfrac12\,\rho\,A\,V^2\!\left[ C_D(\theta)\,\hat{\mathbf{v}} + C_N(\theta)\,\operatorname{sgn}\!\big(\mathbf{n}\!\cdot\!\hat{\mathbf{v}}\big)\,\mathbf{n} \right]. \tag{1}$$ $$\rho$$ $$\boldsymbol{\rho}$$

Aerodynamics

First, we define the velocity of the air at each point in space and time. Call this function v_air(x,t).
Next, we model each leaf as a two-dimensional disk. Under this idealization, we can analytically compute the force and the moment that air exerts on each leaf.
Finally, from classical mechanics we can determine how each leaf will move based on these forces and moments by integrating Newton's second law over time.
